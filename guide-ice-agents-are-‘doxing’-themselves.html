Preventing Unintentional Personal Information Disclosure: A Guide for Online Presence Management

Introduction

The term "doxing themselves" refers to the unintentional or inadvertent public disclosure of an individual's personal or identifying information through their own online activities. While often associated with external malicious actors, individuals can unknowingly contribute to the exposure of their own data. This guide outlines common causes of such disclosures and provides actionable steps to mitigate the risks, ensuring a more secure and private online presence.

3 Common Causes of Unintentional Information Disclosure

  1. Inconsistent Separation of Professional and Personal Online Identities: Using the same identifying markers (e.g., profile pictures, usernames, email addresses, public social media handles) across both professional and personal online accounts makes it easy for others to connect the two, potentially revealing personal information intended to be private.

  2. Default or Improperly Configured Privacy Settings: Many online platforms (social media, forums, professional networking sites) have default privacy settings that are more public than individuals intend. Failure to review and adjust these settings can lead to broad visibility of personal data, posts, or connections.

  3. Unintentional Disclosure Through Shared Content or Metadata: Posting content (e.g., photos, documents, public comments) that contains identifiable personal information, geotagging data, or embedded metadata (such as author name, creation date, or location details in file properties) without realizing its public accessibility.

Step-by-Step Fix: Mitigating Information Disclosure Risks

To prevent unintentional personal information disclosure, implement the following practices:

Step 1: Establish and Maintain Clear Identity Separation

  • Create Distinct Online Personas: Use entirely different usernames, email addresses, and profile pictures for professional accounts versus personal social media or other online platforms.
  • Avoid Cross-Linking: Do not link personal social media profiles directly from professional profiles or vice-versa. Keep personal and professional contacts separate where possible.
  • Segment Digital Activity: Consider using separate web browsers, browser profiles, or even devices for professional versus personal online activities to minimize data bleed.

Step 2: Thoroughly Review and Secure Privacy Settings

  • Audit All Online Accounts: Regularly check and update privacy settings on every online platform you use (e.g., Facebook, X/Twitter, Instagram, LinkedIn, forums, blogs). Assume default settings are public until proven otherwise.
  • Set Most Restrictive Settings First: Opt for the highest privacy settings available (e.g., "Friends Only," "Private Account," "Do Not Share Location") and only loosen them for specific, trusted purposes.
  • Manage Location Services: Disable geotagging on cameras and social media apps unless absolutely necessary. Understand which apps have access to your location data and restrict as appropriate.
  • Enable Two-Factor Authentication (2FA): Activate 2FA on all accounts that offer it to add an extra layer of security, even if your password is compromised.

Step 3: Practice Content Prudence and Metadata Awareness

  • "Think Before You Post": Before sharing any content (text, images, videos), consider who might see it and what information it could reveal. Assume anything posted online could become permanent and public.
  • Scrutinize Visual Content: Before uploading photos, carefully review the background, visible documents, personal items, or unique landmarks that could reveal your location, identity, or associations.
  • Remove Metadata from Files: Use tools to strip metadata from documents (e.g., Word, PDF) and images before sharing them publicly. This includes information like author name, creation date, and location.
  • Be Mindful of Public Interactions: When engaging in public online discussions, forums, or comment sections, be aware that your contributions can be linked to your public identity. Avoid sharing personal details in these contexts.

Step 4: Conduct Regular Self-Audits

  • Search Your Own Name: Periodically perform searches for your own name, usernames, and professional affiliations using different search engines (e.g., Google, Bing) and in incognito/private browsing mode to see what information is publicly accessible.
  • Check "People Finder" Sites: Review "people finder" or public record websites to see what information they list about you and take steps to request removal if data is inaccurate or unwanted.
  • Review Connected Apps: Check the "connected apps" or "authorized apps" section in your social media and email accounts to revoke access for services you no longer use or don't recognize.

Conclusion

Preventing unintentional personal information disclosure is an ongoing process that requires vigilance and proactive management of your digital footprint. By consistently separating your professional and personal online identities, configuring robust privacy settings, exercising caution with shared content, and performing regular self-audits, you can significantly reduce the risk of inadvertently revealing sensitive personal information and enhance your overall online security.
